Scholarly work on Palestine-Israel can struggle to reach beyond its primary readership due to the political reality on the ground. However, the SHP builds on a growing body of work by scholars and political thinkers, in Palestine-Israel and elsewhere, who have been developing the paradigm of partnership over recent years. What the project tries to add is reach: ensuring the work lands beyond academic circles and into the conversations and decisions that actually shape the region's future. 

That starts with civil society. The SHP treats Palestinian and Israeli civil society organisations not as eventual audiences for the project's outputs but as partners whose input shapes the work as it develops. Their role is to bring the questions and concerns that fine-tune the project's recommendations and help address its blind spots, and to serve as a bridge to the wider publics whose lives the work is about. 

The project also engages other audiences whose work shapes how the future of Palestine-Israel is imagined and decided. With policy and political audiences across the United Nations, the European Union, the United States, and the Arab League, the aim is to shift their attachment to separation-based paradigms, which would itself alter the power dynamics in the region. With progressive media, think tanks, and content creators, the aim is to disrupt and shift the dominant discourse on Palestine-Israel. With academics and students, the aim is to carry the ideas forward across disciplines and into the next generation of scholarship. 

At the same time, the SHP is neither a peace plan to be tabled at a summit nor a blueprint that any government will adopt wholesale. Its impact will be cumulative and contested, and will depend on the work of civil society, policymakers, scholars, and journalists who take up, contest, and build on what the project produces. That is the nature of work like this, and it is why the project is built as a partnership.
