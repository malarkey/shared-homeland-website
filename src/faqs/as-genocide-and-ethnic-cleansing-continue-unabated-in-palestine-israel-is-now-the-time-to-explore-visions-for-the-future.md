After decades of failed peace processes and now in the midst of a genocide, the very vocabulary of "solutions" and "visions" has come to feel suspect to many Palestinians and to others working in solidarity with them. The concern is understandable: when bodies are being pulled from rubble in Gaza, when settler violence is displacing communities in the West Bank, East Jerusalem and the Naqab/Negev, when the catastrophe is ongoing, what business does anyone have writing about some distant future? 

We take that concern seriously. The SHP is not a substitute for the urgent work of stopping the violence, defending Palestinian lives and rights, prosecuting those responsible, and confronting the structures that have produced this moment. Those struggles are immediate and significant. But the question of what comes after is already being asked and requires convincing answers. The political imagination that gets to that question first will shape what is possible. If the dominant framework remains separation and domination, then the next chapter will not look meaningfully different from the present one. The point of working on the SHP now is to ensure that when the political opening comes, a substantively different vision is available: one grounded in equality, mutual self-determination, and decolonisation rather than in the management of permanent injustice. 

Our work is done with the past and present in view. Any honest and sustainable vision for the future of Palestine-Israel must engage the wrongs of the past and the catastrophes of the present: the Nakba, the occupation, the genocide. These cannot be treated as background; they are the starting condition. A future that does not acknowledge what has been done cannot be a just one. The project's commitment to decolonisation is in part an insistence on this: that the structures producing today's violence cannot be carried into whatever comes next.
