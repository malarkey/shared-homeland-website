Yes. The SHP is built on principles that international law has long affirmed, and in several important respects it gives those principles fuller effect than existing peace frameworks have managed. 

The foundations are familiar. Two sovereign states on borders based on the 1967 line. The end of occupation, which the International Court of Justice ruled unlawful in its entirety in its July 2024 advisory opinion. The dismantling of the settlement enterprise, which that same opinion required Israel to cease and to undo. The right of Palestinian refugees to return to their homes, guaranteed by human rights law, affirmed in UN resolutions, and reaffirmed by the ICJ. The right of both peoples to national self-determination, foundational in the UN Charter and the major human rights treaties.  The SHP framework takes these commitments seriously enough to insist on their realisation. 

Some elements of the framework are innovative but not unprecedented. The decoupling of citizenship from residency draws on regional arrangements like the Schengen Area and the EU citizenship regime. Joint institutions for transboundary governance have precedents across Europe and beyond.
