This is one of the most challenging questions the project is addressing. The settlements in the West Bank were established and have been maintained and expanded in patent violation of international law. They are an Israeli state colonial project, built and sustained through decades of governmental policy, military protection and financial support, and designed explicitly to make Palestinian self-determination harder to achieve. They sit at the heart of the system of occupation and Jewish supremacy that the SHP directly challenges. At the same time, they are home to some 700,000 Israelis whose forced relocation would present formidable political, security, economic, and human costs. Forced relocation on that scale could also reinforce a logic of separation, reprising the catastrophic population transfers of the 20th century and deepening rather than diminishing ethnic conflict. 

Under the SHP the West Bank becomes part of the sovereign territory of the State of Palestine. The occupation ends, and the state project of settlement ends with it. With those structures gone, the categories themselves change. Settlements cease to be settlements and become residential communities open to Palestinians and subject to Palestinian law. Settlers, in turn, cease to be settlers. Some Israelis who live in the West Bank may have a presumptive option to remain as permanent residents of the State of Palestine. Under this arrangement, they keep their Israeli citizenship and their national political rights inside Israel and gain local residency rights in Palestine, but do not vote in Palestinian national elections.  

The project's principal challenge is to find arrangements that redress one set of injustices without producing new ones, delivering the structural redress owed to Palestinians dispossessed by the settlements while protecting the safety and dignity of both peoples. This is not vindication of the settlement enterprise. It is its dismantling. The legal, military, and demographic machinery of Jewish supremacy that produced settler status in the occupied territory ends. What comes next must address the injustice that system caused: through Palestinian refugees return, through restitution, through transitional justice.  Decolonisation, in this project, is not about replacing one people's dominance with another's, it is about ending domination as a relationship between Israelis and Palestinians.
